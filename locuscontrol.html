The Locus of Control

is the general belief that an individual's successes, failures and outcomes are either controlled by their own actions or by other forces such as chance, luck and fate. (P. Spector, 1988).

"When you are dealing with a challenge in your life, do you feel that you have control over the outcome? Or do you believe that you are simply at the hands of outside forces?"

However
the locus of control exists on a continuum.

No one has a 100% external or internal locus of control. Instead, most people lie somewhere on the continuum between the two extremes.

Internal Locus of Control

refers to one's belief that they have control over their own fate and experiences

It refers to when individuals believe that outcomes and their success and failures are the result of their own actions and efforts, and are able to control and manage their own lives by making decisions about the events, whilst having the ability to determine their own outcomes and being responsible for what happened.

External locus of control

refers to one's belief that they have no control over what happens and that external variables or factors are to blame

It refers to when individuals believe that 'external' factors such as fate, chance, and luck, friends, and managers determine the outcomes (successes or failures) which they themselves experience.

Where do I exist on the continuum?

Below are characteristics of people who exist on different points along the continuum


Someone with a high internal locus of control

Pamela, a 20 years old undergraduate student, received an “A+” for her Final Statistics examination. She believes that she did very well because she spent 3 hours every night for a month to practise and revise. It was her hard work and effort that paid off.


Someone with a balanced internal & external locus of control

Jacob, a 21 years old undergraduate, received an “A-” for his Final Statistic grade. He believes that it was a results of his hard work, effort and his awesome groupmates. They worked hard together for their final project thereafter, they also helped him in areas that he did not understand.


Someone with a high external locus of control

Andrew, 21 years old, undergraduate believes that he received a “C-” in his statistics exams because his professor does not like him and also, because he was late for the exam due to the heavy downpour on the exam day.

Remember that you always have the ability to change your situation.

There is no situation that you are in that you can’t improve through your own personal action. Sure, it might seem hopeless at the moment, but there are better roads forward from here, no matter what the situation.

Make a list of possible actions
you can take in response to a difficult situation.

Everyone faces serious challenges at some point in their life. Rather than looking at that challenge as an impenetrable wall, instead look for things you can to to handle this difficult situation.

Stop focusing on things that you cannot control about a situation, and when you catch yourself doing it, think instead about the aspects of the situation that you can control.

There are factors in every situation that you can’t control. You can’t control the personalities and responses of others. You can’t control natural events or physical accidents. So stop worrying about those things, because you can’t do much about them. Instead, look at what you can control – your responses to those situations, how you handle your emotions, and the daily choices you make. The better your responses and choices, the less impact those negative things you can’t control will have.
